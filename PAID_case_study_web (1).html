Overview

Preserving African architecture before it disappears

PAID (Pan-African Indigenous Skills Development Programme) is a training and cultural preservation initiative focused on revitalising African architectural heritage before it is lost to urbanisation and generational disconnect. The 2026 pilot phase, themed Roots and Roofs, centred on African-inspired interior and exterior architecture.

The programme was delivered by CBAAC (Centre for Black and African Arts and Civilisation) under the EUNIC Spaces of Culture framework, with co-implementing partners including the Goethe-Institut, British Council Nigeria, Irish Embassy Lagos, Spanish Embassy Lagos, the Institute of African and Diaspora Studies (IADS), and the EU Delegation Nigeria.

I joined as Programme Communications Coordinator at the start of the pilot phase, responsible for building and managing the coordination infrastructure that connected every layer of the programme from partner organisations to individual participants.

Programme Structure

A two-stage selection process across a complex stakeholder ecosystem

The programme recruited young Nigerian creatives in paired teams: one architect or designer and one photographer or visual storyteller, with one team selected from each of Nigeria's six geopolitical zones plus the FCT. The selection process ran in two stages.

Stage 1: Steering Committee

  • 12 committee members drawn from partner organisations across 6 zones
  • Each zone produced a longlist of applicants for review
  • Members drawn from Goethe-Institut, British Council, Irish Embassy, Spanish Embassy, IADS, National Troupe, CBAAC, and the EU Delegation

Stage 2: Final Jury

  • 5-member international jury reviewed shortlisted applications
  • 2 independent expert practitioners: Chadrac Agbodjogbe and Otitioluwa Tubi
  • 3 Steering Committee representatives: CBAAC, IADS, and Irish Embassy Lagos

Facilitators

  • Approximately 15 confirmed facilitators with onboarded documentation
  • Split across 9 virtual and 6 physical sessions
  • Full programme roster extended to approximately 25 including regional specialists

Participants

  • 24 participants selected (12 creative teams of 2)
  • One team per geopolitical zone plus FCT
  • Each team: one architect/designer paired with one photographer/visual storyteller
My Contribution

Building the coordination infrastructure from scratch

The scope of my role extended well beyond communications. I was responsible for designing and running the operational backbone that kept the programme connected across its many moving parts simultaneously.

01

Coordination Infrastructure

Built independently from scratch: a facilitator tracking system, a participant contact database organised by geopolitical zone, and a full email template library covering every recurring communication type across stakeholder groups.

02

Facilitator Journey Management

Managed the full facilitator lifecycle from sourcing through to offboarding: onboarding, session scheduling, contract processing, invoice management per Goethe-Institut financial requirements, travel logistics, and document compliance tracking.

03

Multi-Stakeholder Communications

Maintained distinct and simultaneous communication streams for four stakeholder groups: facilitators, participants, jury members, and partner organisation representatives. Each group required tailored communications at different frequencies and formats.

04

Reporting and Documentation

Produced status reports for programme leadership throughout the engagement. Created comprehensive handover documentation upon contract conclusion, ensuring full programme continuity with zero information loss for the team continuing delivery.

Key Challenges

What made this genuinely difficult

Stakeholder density at every level. At any given moment I was managing communications across partner organisations, a 12-person steering committee, a 5-member international jury, 15+ facilitators split across virtual and physical tracks, and 24 individual participants spread across six geopolitical zones. Each group had different information needs, response expectations, and communication formats.

The programme infrastructure did not exist when I joined. There was no central tracking system, no standardised contact database, and no template library for the recurring communications that a programme of this scale requires. Building those systems while simultaneously running active communications was the defining challenge of the role.

Working across partner organisations with different institutional cultures, each with their own internal processes, also required careful calibration. Communications to the Irish Embassy had different expectations than communications to CBAAC or to individual participant teams. Getting that right consistently, at volume, required the template system I built to earn its value.

Reflection

What this project taught me

The PAID programme reinforced something I believe about coordination work at scale: the infrastructure you build before delivery begins determines whether delivery is possible at all. The tracking systems, the templates, the contact databases — none of those are visible in the final output, but without them the communications would have become unmanageable within weeks.

This project also clarified how important it is to understand which stakeholder needs accuracy and which needs speed. A jury member and a participant have completely different relationships to the programme and completely different tolerances for ambiguity in communications. Calibrating for that difference at volume is a skill I developed here.

The contract concluded on my side before the programme's full delivery. I completed a structured handover that documented every active process, contact, and outstanding item, ensuring the team continuing the work had everything they needed to proceed without disruption.
