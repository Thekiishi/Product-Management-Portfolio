The Scenario

50 enterprise clients, 90 days, zero tolerance for data loss

TechCorp has acquired a modern SaaS platform and must migrate 50 enterprise clients from a legacy system within 90 days. The legacy system has become a liability: frequent downtime, limited content management, poor SEO performance, and mounting security vulnerabilities are putting client relationships at risk.

The planning challenge: design a migration approach that protects $450K in active client contracts, maintains zero data loss across all migrations, and keeps client downtime within SLA limits — while coordinating a cross-functional team of 15 across engineering, client success, QA, and content.

Stakeholder Analysis

Mapping everyone with a stake in delivery

Internal Stakeholders

  • Engineering: technical migration execution and data integrity
  • Client Success: primary client contacts throughout the migration
  • QA: pre and post-migration validation and sign-off
  • Content: redirects, SEO preservation, and content migration
  • Executive leadership: budget authority and escalation path

External Stakeholders

  • 50 enterprise clients: end-recipients of migration, varying technical capacity
  • Named contacts per account: primary communication point per migration
  • Third-party integrations: client-side tools requiring reconnection post-migration

Key insight: Enterprise clients have widely varying technical capacity. A single migration playbook would not work. The communication and support approach needed to be tiered by client complexity, not uniform across all accounts.

Phased Delivery Plan

Three phases across 11 weeks

The migration is designed as a phased rollout with one week of buffer against the 12-week deadline, allowing for controlled risk at each stage before committing the full client base.

Weeks 1–3

Phase 1: Preparation and Pilot

Audit all 50 client environments. Complete redirect mapping and SEO preservation planning. Select 5 lower-complexity clients for a controlled pilot migration. Full validation and feedback gathered before proceeding.

Weeks 4–8

Phase 2: Scaled Migration

Migrate remaining 45 clients in tiered batches by complexity. All migrations outside business hours. Each client receives advance notice, a plain-language briefing, and a named point of contact. 48-hour structured follow-up per client post-migration.

Weeks 9–11

Phase 3: Validation and Stabilisation

30-day post-migration monitoring for SEO, performance, and issue reports. Resolve outstanding client issues. Formal project close and retrospective. One week of buffer held against week 12.

Planning Targets

What the plan was designed to achieve

These targets were built into the planning documentation as success criteria for the migration programme. They represent the intended outcomes the plan was structured to deliver, not post-delivery results.

Delivery Targets

  • 11-week delivery against a 12-week schedule
  • One week of buffer maintained throughout
  • Zero critical incidents as the target threshold

Budget Targets

  • 8% headroom below the $250K ceiling
  • 15% contingency reserve built in and preserved
  • No scope additions without formal change control

Client Success Targets

  • 92% client satisfaction as the defined success threshold
  • Zero data loss across all 50 migrations
  • All downtime within agreed SLA limits

Reflection

What this brief demonstrates

The value of working to a live brief under real pressure is that the thinking cannot be approximate. Building a risk register before you know what will go wrong, writing a communication plan before you have met the clients, designing a phased timeline with deliberate buffer before delivery pressures compress it — these are the habits that distinguish proactive project management from reactive coordination.

The stakeholder tiering approach came from working through the brief carefully rather than defaulting to a generic structure. That specificity in planning is what the work was designed to surface and what a hiring team is looking for when they set a brief like this.
